    Binary vs. decimal units

    Data sizes use two competing definitions — and mismatching them is the reason your 1 TB drive shows up as ~931 GB in your OS.

    • SI / Decimal (KB, MB, GB, TB) — powers of 1000. Defined by the International System of Units. Used by hard drive manufacturers, network speeds, and storage vendors because the numbers look bigger.
    • IEC / Binary (KiB, MiB, GiB, TiB) — powers of 1024. Defined by IEC 80000-13. Used by RAM, operating systems (historically), and low-level tools because memory addresses are powers of 2.

    The gap grows with scale: 1 GB = 1,000,000,000 bytes; 1 GiB = 1,073,741,824 bytes — a ~7.4% difference. A "1 TB" drive holds 1,000,000,000,000 bytes, which is only ~931 GiB.

    Network speeds are always in bits (lowercase b), not bytes. A 100 Mbps connection transfers 100 megabits per second = 12.5 megabytes per second.
