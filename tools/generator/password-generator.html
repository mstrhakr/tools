    How it works & why it matters

    Passwords are generated entirely in your browser using crypto.getRandomValues() — a cryptographically secure pseudo-random number generator (CSPRNG). Math.random() is never used; it is predictable and not suitable for security.

    Length beats complexity. NIST SP 800-63B recommends prioritizing length over mandatory character-set rules. The math explains why: a 16-character lowercase-only password has roughly 75 bits of entropy; adding uppercase, digits, and symbols to an 8-character password yields only ~52 bits. Every character you add multiplies the keyspace far more than adding a new character class does.

    • At 16+ characters, even modest character sets are outside the reach of current brute-force hardware.
    • At 8 characters with full complexity, a modern GPU cluster can exhaust the entire space in hours to days.
    • Passphrases (long, memorable phrases like correct-horse-battery-staple) combine high entropy with low friction — NIST recommends them over short complex passwords.
    • NIST no longer recommends forced periodic rotation — it encourages predictable, weaker changes (Summer2024! → Summer2025!).

    The strength meter estimates entropy as log₂(charset_sizelength). Expanding the charset from lowercase-only (~26) to all printable ASCII (~94) adds about 0.6 bits per character — one extra character adds ~6.6 bits. Length wins every time.
