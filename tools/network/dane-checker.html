    What is DANE?

    DNS-based Authentication of Named Entities (DANE) is a security protocol defined in RFC 6698 that lets domain owners publish TLS certificate constraints directly in DNS, via TLSA records. This allows a connecting client to verify a server's certificate against what the domain owner has published — without relying solely on a Certificate Authority (CA).

    How TLSA Records Work

    A TLSA record is published at a well-known owner name derived from the service endpoint:

    _<port>._<protocol>.<hostname>

    For example, to protect SMTP on port 25 over TCP for example.com, the TLSA record lives at _25._tcp.example.com. Each record contains three fields:

    • Certificate Usage — how to use the referenced certificate material (pinned CA, pinned end-entity, etc.).
    • Selector — whether the full certificate or just the public key is matched.
    • Matching Type — whether the record stores the raw data or a SHA-256/SHA-512 hash of it.

    Why It Matters

    DANE is primarily used to harden SMTP (email) delivery via DANE-SMTP (RFC 7672), preventing downgrade and man-in-the-middle attacks that ordinary STARTTLS is vulnerable to. It is also applicable to HTTPS and other TLS services. Because TLSA records are signed with DNSSEC, the published certificate constraints are tamper-evident — an attacker would have to compromise both DNS and the CA ecosystem to forge a connection.

    Scoring

    • +1 — DNS resolver returned a successful (NOERROR) status for the TLSA query name.
    • +1 — At least one TLSA record was found for the endpoint.

    A score of 2/2 means the endpoint has a valid, published TLSA record. A score of 0 or 1 means DANE is not fully configured for this service.
