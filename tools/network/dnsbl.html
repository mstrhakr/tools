    How This Works

    DNS-based Blackhole Lists use a clever DNS trick: to check if 1.2.3.4 is listed on zen.spamhaus.org, the backend queries 4.3.2.1.zen.spamhaus.org — the IP reversed and prepended to the DNSBL zone. An A record response (e.g., 127.0.0.2) means listed; NXDOMAIN means clean. The specific return code encodes the reason for listing. Browsers can't perform raw DNS queries, so lookups run server-side and results across multiple lists are aggregated simultaneously.

    Common blacklists checked:

    • Spamhaus ZEN — the industry standard; covers known spam sources, hijacked IPs, and compromised hosts.
    • Barracuda BRBL — focused on email spam sources.
    • SORBS — covers spam originators and open proxies.
    • SpamCop — based on user-submitted spam reports.

    Being listed doesn't always mean active malicious activity — residential IPs are often listed due to a previous user, and cloud/VPS IPs are frequently listed preemptively. Contact the blacklist operator's removal process to delist.
